Spatio-Temporal Distribution of Burned Areas

Politecnico di Milano · CNR-IREA · Geoinformatics Engineering

Authors: Rishikesh Miriyala · Sriram Krishnasamy Seenivasan · Ghulam Abbas Zafari

Abstract

This study compares burned area estimates from global datasets (FireCCI54 and MCD64) using Google Earth Engine. Multi-year analysis reveals spatial and temporal patterns, evaluates the influence of climate and land use, and integrates various environmental indicators to enhance fire risk assessment and dataset reliability.

Keywords

Burned Area, Spatio-Temporal Analysis, FireCCI54, MCD64, Google Earth Engine, NDVI, LULC, Forest Loss.

Introduction

Wildfires have far-reaching effects. While satellite products like FireCCI51 and MCD64 provide burned area estimates, discrepancies exist. This work uses Google Earth Engine to resolve these issues, comparing datasets and incorporating external factors like vegetation health and climate patterns.

Methods

2.1 Data Sources

Burned Area products used: FireCCI54 and MCD64.

2.2 Active Fire Detection

VIIRS fire points classified into five intensity levels.

2.3 Land Surface Temperature

MODIS daily LST analyzed to identify heat anomalies in fire-prone zones.

2.4 LULC Classification

Land cover types measured against fire occurrence and spread.

2.5 NDVI Anomalies

Vegetation health trends before and after fire incidents via NDVI.

2.6 Forest Loss

Quantified forest depletion using Hansen’s dataset.

2.7 Environmental Variables

Analyzed elevation, soil moisture, temperature, and wind as fire predictors.

Results

3.1 Burned Area Estimates

Differences in BA location and intensity between FireCCI54 and MCD64 were mapped and compared.

3.2 Fire Classifications

Fire intensities showed biome-specific clustering; savannas and forests were particularly vulnerable.

3.3 NDVI & Temperature Trends

NDVI anomalies matched fire timing. LST rose notably during peak fire months.

3.4 Land Use Insights

Agricultural zones faced higher burn rates, while urban regions remained mostly unaffected.

3.5 Predictors and Visualization

Combined models linked fire patterns to moisture, elevation, and temperature. Burned masks helped in detailed spatial interpretation.

Discussion

Integrating GEE with multiple datasets strengthened analysis consistency. Dataset disagreements revealed the need for standard methods. Predictive modeling showed promise in enhancing fire monitoring tools.

Conclusion

GEE enabled a scalable, accurate approach to burned area analysis. The study strengthens fire management strategies and opens paths for advanced predictive modeling using environmental factors.
